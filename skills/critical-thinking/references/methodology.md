# Methodology: Critical Thinking

This reference distills practical critical-thinking principles for coaching users through claims, views, arguments, decisions, and phenomena.

## Core Frame

Critical thinking is thinking about thinking. It evaluates a conclusion, belief, decision, or interpretation by asking whether the reasoning meets good standards.

The aim is not to be negative, win an argument, or reject everything. The aim is to form a more reasonable judgment, avoid bad arguments, resist irrelevant emotional pressure, and improve the quality of the user's own thinking.

## Starting Point

Begin with something concrete:

- A personal view.
- A claim from someone else.
- A public statement.
- A decision or recommendation.
- A phenomenon that invites interpretation.
- A dispute, policy, plan, product idea, or situation.

Do not run the method on an empty topic. If no view or phenomenon is supplied, ask for one.

## Argument Anatomy

Most critical-thinking work starts by separating:

- `Claim`: what is being asserted, concluded, interpreted, predicted, or recommended.
- `Reasons`: why someone thinks the claim is true or worth accepting.
- `Evidence`: facts, examples, data, testimony, observations, or experience used to support the reasons.
- `Assumptions`: unstated premises that must be true for the reasoning to work.
- `Implications`: what follows if the claim is accepted.

Use a compact argument map:

`Claim -> Reasons -> Evidence -> Assumptions`

If the user starts with a phenomenon, first identify the interpretation or conclusion they are tempted to draw from it.

## Facts, Opinions, And Values

Separate categories before judging:

- Factual claims can be checked.
- Opinions or interpretations explain what facts mean.
- Value judgments depend on standards, priorities, ethics, or taste.
- Predictions require causal or probabilistic support.
- Recommendations require both factual and value premises.

Many weak arguments blur these categories. Clarifying the category often reveals what kind of evidence is needed.

## Evidence Standards

Use these standards to evaluate support:

- Relevance: the evidence bears on the claim.
- Sufficiency: the amount and variety of evidence fit the strength of the conclusion.
- Credibility: the source has competence, reliability, access, and low enough bias.
- Representativeness: samples or examples are not cherry-picked.
- Precision: key terms, numbers, comparisons, and time ranges are clear.
- Consistency: the evidence does not contradict itself without explanation.
- Alternatives: the evidence is compared against rival explanations.

Avoid accepting broad conclusions from thin examples. Avoid rejecting a claim only because evidence is incomplete; sometimes the right output is lower confidence or a narrower conclusion.

## Common Reasoning Risks

Check for these only when relevant:

- Confirmation bias: filtering evidence to protect an existing belief.
- Availability bias: overweighting vivid, recent, or emotionally memorable examples.
- Anchoring: staying too close to the first frame, number, or story.
- Motivated reasoning: using reasoning to defend a desired answer.
- Authority pressure: treating authority as decisive when domain competence or source incentives are unclear.
- Group pressure: accepting a belief because the group treats it as obvious.
- False dilemma: treating two options as the only options.
- Hasty generalization: moving from a small or unrepresentative sample to a broad conclusion.
- Straw man: replacing an opposing view with an easier target.
- Ad hominem: attacking a person instead of the claim.
- Slippery slope: asserting a chain of consequences without supporting the links.
- Equivocation: shifting the meaning of a key term.
- Loaded language: using emotional language as if it were evidence.

Name a bias or fallacy only when you can tie it to a specific reasoning move. Otherwise describe the risk plainly.

## Strong Opposition

A conclusion is stronger when it survives a good opposing version.

Use:

- Steelman: state the best opposing argument, not the weakest.
- Alternative explanation: ask what else could explain the same evidence.
- Missing evidence: ask what fact would make the conclusion false or too broad.
- Stakeholder shift: ask what another affected party would notice.
- Scope narrowing: ask where the claim holds and where it does not.

## Calibrated Judgment

Critical thinking should end with a calibrated result, not automatic certainty.

Possible outcomes:

- Accept the claim with stated confidence.
- Reject the claim with reasons.
- Suspend judgment until evidence improves.
- Narrow the claim to a context where support is adequate.
- Revise the claim into a more precise version.
- Separate a true factual part from a questionable interpretation.

Always include what evidence would change the judgment.
