# Methodology: Why / What If / How

This reference distills the core questioning arc from Warren Berger's *A More Beautiful Question* / *如何提出一个好问题* for use in this skill.

## Core Frame

A good question is ambitious but executable. It changes how people perceive or think about a situation and can become a catalyst for action.

The Why / What if / How arc is a thinking framework, not a rigid formula:

- **Why** creates insight. Step back from the surface problem, question assumptions, and identify the right problem to solve.
- **What if** creates imagination. Suspend judgment, combine ideas, and generate possible futures or solution hypotheses.
- **How** creates execution. Turn one promising possibility into action, experiments, and practical commitments.

## Why Principles

Use Why to avoid solving the wrong problem. The assistant's job is to guide the user from symptoms toward the deeper issue.

The Why phase should be guided mainly by Toyota 5 Whys logic:

- **Berger's Why**: step back from the given problem, question assumptions, and look for a more meaningful question.
- **Toyota 5 Whys**: trace cause behind cause until a more fundamental reason becomes visible. This is not a script that repeats the word "why"; it is a discipline for making every follow-up question depend on the previous answer and dig deeper into the real cause.
- **First-principles thinking**: use only as a supporting lens when useful. It can help strip away inherited assumptions and ask what must be true at a basic level, but it should not force the conversation into a rigid framework.

Flexible deepening principle:

Each new Why question should be one step deeper than the last. Depending on the user's answer, that deeper step may point toward a process, a habit, a missing resource, an incentive, a constraint, a mistaken assumption, a conflicting goal, a user need, or a first-principles contradiction. Do not require all conversations to pass through the same categories.

Useful question patterns:

- Ask why the stated problem matters now.
- Ask "What has to be true for this symptom to keep happening?"
- Ask "What process, incentive, or constraint makes this outcome likely?"
- Ask "Why is this workaround, habit, or tradeoff considered acceptable?"
- Ask "Which part is a real constraint, and which part is inherited convention?"
- Ask "What goal, fear, constraint, incentive, or habit sits behind the problem?"
- Ask "Is the current formulation too narrow, too solution-shaped, or too focused on symptoms?"
- Ask what would change if the deeper problem were solved.

Weak Why questions are generic, motivational, or detached from the user's last answer. Strong Why questions name a specific causal claim from the user's answer and push one level deeper.

Examples of stronger forms:

- Instead of "Why is this important?", ask "You said the team delays decisions because the data is unclear. Why does unclear data stop decisions instead of triggering a bounded experiment?"
- Instead of "Why does this happen?", ask "What in the current workflow makes this failure the predictable outcome rather than an exception?"
- Instead of "Why do users not use it?", ask "What user need or cost is the product assuming incorrectly?"

The final root question should be:

- More fundamental than the user's starting question.
- Still actionable by the user or their organization.
- Open enough to inspire multiple solution paths.
- Specific enough to guide practical choices.
- Framed around the most fundamental reason uncovered during the Why chain rather than a surface symptom.

## What If Principles

Use What if to delay judgment and expand possibility before choosing a path.

Useful moves:

- Recombine existing ideas, resources, people, or contexts in unusual ways.
- Invert an assumption: "What if the constraint became the design principle?"
- Borrow from another field or role: "What if this were handled like a product launch, a classroom, a clinic, a game, or a community?"
- Remove a limiting belief temporarily: "What if budget, status, policy, or habit were not the first constraint?"
- Create options that can later be tested, not just imagined.

The assistant's 3 examples should be diverse enough to help the user think beyond obvious fixes.

## How Principles

Use How to move from possibility to disciplined action. Prefer "How might we..." style framing because it assumes a solution is possible, keeps judgment open, and invites collaboration.

Implementation planning should:

- Convert the chosen hypothesis into a concrete goal.
- Identify the smallest useful experiment.
- Make assumptions testable.
- Define success criteria.
- Surface risks without killing momentum.
- Include next actions, owners or roles, resources, and a realistic sequence.

Do not pretend the plan is definitive. Keep it useful, adaptable, and tied to the user's context.
