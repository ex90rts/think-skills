# system-thinking

`system-thinking` is an interactive systems-thinking skill. It helps users move beyond isolated single-point analysis and understand a problem, plan, or decision through variables, causal chains, reinforcing loops, balancing loops, delay effects, results, and stakeholders.

Use it when a problem is complex, recurring, cross-functional, delayed, or shaped by multiple actors.

## Best For

- Analyzing complex problems that cannot be explained by one cause.
- Evaluating whether a plan or solution is feasible.
- Mapping causes, results, entities, and stakeholders.
- Finding reinforcing loops that amplify success or failure.
- Finding balancing loops that resist change or cap growth.
- Spotting delay effects that hide the real outcome.
- Identifying leverage points for intervention.

## Workflow

1. **Intake**: collect the problem, background, current plan, and desired analysis goal.
2. **System boundary**: define what is inside and outside the system.
3. **Stakeholders and results**: identify affected entities and intended or unintended outcomes.
4. **Variables**: convert vague factors into observable variables.
5. **Causal chains**: connect variables with directional cause-effect links.
6. **Reinforcing loops**: find cycles that amplify growth, decline, escalation, or lock-in.
7. **Balancing loops**: find cycles that stabilize, resist, or limit change.
8. **Delay effects**: identify time gaps that distort judgment.
9. **Intervention**: evaluate or design actions based on leverage points.
10. **Synthesis**: produce a concise system map and recommendation.

## Notes

This skill should not stop at a factor list. It should show relationships, loops, delays, and stakeholder effects so important system elements are not missed.
